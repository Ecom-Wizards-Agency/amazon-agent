# Catalog SOP: Pesticide Claims-Keywords to Avoid

## Pesticide Claims-Keywords to Avoid

**Who is this for :** This document is intended for individuals who are involved in creating or reviewing advertising materials, copywriting, or any other things that prohibited keywords can be used. Its purpose is to provide guidance on avoiding certain keywords in pesticide claims, in order to ensure that such claims are accurate, truthful, and comply with applicable regulations.

**Objective:** This SOP aims to educate the readers about some specific keywords or phrases that imply or suggest that a product can be used to control pests, as per EPA regulations, in any text, backend, or image on Amazon detail pages, to prevent misclassification of the product as a pesticide. To learn more, visit the [Pest control products and pesticides](https://sellercentral.amazon.com/help/hub/reference/G202115120) help page.

What is a [Pesticide](https://www.epa.gov/minimum-risk-pesticides/what-pesticide)?

- Any substance or mixture of substances intended for preventing, destroying, repelling, or mitigating any pest.
- Any substance or mixture of substances intended for use as a plant regulator, defoliant, or desiccant.
- Any nitrogen stabilizer.

**Note:**

- **Any claim to prevent, destroy, repel, or mitigate any pest will be considered a pesticide claim.**
- **Also the word "****pesticide****" is Prohibited.**

**The following keywords are pesticide claims but are not limited to:**

- Anti-Microbial
- Anti-Bacterial
- Anti-Fungal
- Antiseptic
- Disinfect
- Flea
- Hypo-Allergenic
- Mildew
- Non-toxic
- Prevents Bacteria
- Rabbits
- Repellants
- Rodent
- Spider
- Sterilize
- Tick

FAQs

1. **How do I identify and report a potential pesticide policy violation on my listing?**

- If your listing is flagged for a pesticide policy violation, you'll usually receive an email notification or see a status update in Seller Central. If your listing is deactivated due to a pesticide claim, you can follow the instructions in the "How to Fix Pesticide Yanks and Gating" SOP for further guidance on resolving the issue.

1. **How do I ensure compliance with pesticide-related keywords in backend keywords and images?**

- Backend keywords and image metadata must also be free from any prohibited pesticide-related terms. Review both the text and any hidden metadata or alt-text associated with your images to ensure no pesticide claims are implied. The same prohibited keywords apply across all content types on your product page.

1. **What does it mean to "mitigate" a pest, and can I use that phrase in my product description?**

- "Mitigating" a pest refers to taking actions that reduce or lessen the impact of pests. Claims such as "mitigates pests" or "helps reduce pests" can be considered pesticide claims. Avoid using such language in your product description, backend keywords, and images unless the product is specifically registered as a pesticide.

1. **What should I do if my listing has been flagged for a pesticide violation?**

- If your listing is flagged for a pesticide claim violation, you'll need to revise your product listing by removing any prohibited keywords or claims. You can submit a plan of action to Amazon explaining the changes made to bring the listing into compliance. Refer to the "How to Fix Pesticide Yanks and Gating" SOP for specific instructions on how to address the issue.

1. **Why is "Non-toxic" listed as a prohibited keyword?**

- "Non-toxic" is considered a prohibited keyword because it could imply that a product is safe to use as a pesticide or pest-repellent. While "non-toxic" may be accurate for some products, it can also be misleading and be perceived as a claim that the product has pest control properties. It's best to avoid this term if it could imply pesticide use.

1. **Can I use phrases like “keeps pests away” or “for pest-free homes” in my product listing?**

- Claims like “keeps pests away” or “for pest-free homes” could be interpreted as pesticide claims and may result in policy violations. If you're uncertain whether a phrase constitutes a pesticide claim, it's safer to avoid it or consult Amazon's policy to ensure compliance.

1. **What are the consequences of violating the pesticide policy with prohibited keywords?**

- Violating the pesticide policy can lead to the deactivation of your listing or even the suspension of your selling account, depending on the severity of the violation. It’s important to review Amazon’s policies regularly and ensure that your product listings remain compliant to avoid penalties.

1. **What are some compliant alternative keywords to use instead of prohibited pesticide claims?**

- Instead of using terms like "anti-bacterial" or "repellent," you can focus on neutral, descriptive keywords that highlight the product’s benefits without suggesting pesticide properties. For example, instead of "anti-fungal," you could use "prevents mold growth" or "helps with cleaning." Always focus on providing clear, non-deceptive claims that align with your product’s intended use and Amazon’s policies.
