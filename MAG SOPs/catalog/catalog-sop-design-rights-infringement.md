# Catalog SOP: Design Rights Infringement

Source: https://sop.myamazonguy.com/books/catalog/page/catalog-sop-design-rights-infringement

## **Design Rights Infringement**

**Who is this for :** This is for Amazon UK sellers who may face issues of design rights infringement on their product listings.

**Objective :** This SOP (Standard Operating Procedure) aims to provide guidelines on how to address the infringement of design rights in Amazon UK. It includes steps to take when a design is registered and when permission from the design owner has been obtained, as well as preventive measures to avoid similar incidents.

**SUMMARY:** A design is a form of legal protection for the appearance of the entire product or a part of it which results, in particular, from the characteristics of line, contours, colors, form, surface structure, and/or materials of the product and/or its decoration. Any industrial or handicraft item including packaging, graphic symbols, and typefaces qualifies as a product. Parts of products that can be taken apart and reassembled can also be protected. Infringement of a registered design right occurs when your design is reproduced by a third party. With unregistered protection, you will need to prove that your design has been copied. By registering a design, the owner has a monopoly right in that design. Before you use a design, you should carry out due diligence searches. You should check the existing intellectual property registers to find out if the design belongs to someone else.

You can search for:

- [UK-registered designs](https://www.gov.uk/search-registered-design)
- [EU-registered designs](https://euipo.europa.eu/ohimportal/en/rcd-search-availability)
- [registered industrial designs worldwide](https://www3.wipo.int/designdb/en/index.jsp)

For more information about design rights, see the “Designs” section in this Amazon UK [article](https://sellercentral.amazon.co.uk/help/hub/reference/G201361070).

**How to Fix?**

If you have a design certificate:

- Attach the Certificate in the Plan of Action (POA) or the ticket you're going to send in Seller Central Help.

If you obtained permission from the design owner:

- Attach the licensing agreement or Letter of Authorization (LOA) from the design owner to the POA or the ticket you're going to send in Seller Central Help.

If you don’t have both:

- Design is territorial: a design owner usually protects a design by registering it with a country-specific office[4]. Therefore, delist that listing from that specific marketplace.

POA EXAMPLE (Please tweak them depending on your circumstances)

If you have a design certificate:

Root Cause:

Actions Taken:

1. We’re certified and we own the design in question. Please see the attached document.

Preventive Measures:

1. I’ll make sure that my design registration is up to date. Before it expires, I’ll immediately renew my certificate.

If you obtained permission from the design owner:

Root Cause:

1. ASIN B0XXXXXX was yanked due to Design Rights Infringement of (Product of the infringed brand).

Actions Taken:

1. We read and understood the Amazon Intellectual Property Policy. [https://sellercentral.amazon.co.uk/help/hub/reference/G201361070](https://sellercentral.amazon.co.uk/help/hub/reference/G201361070)
2. We have obtained authorization from the design rights owner. Please see the attached licensing agreement/letter of authorization.
3. I have asked the design rights owner to retract the violation.

Preventive Measures:

1. I’ll make sure that my licensing agreement is up to date. Before it expires, I’ll immediately contact the design's right owner for renewal.
2. I’ll read the Amazon Intellectual Property Policy every month for me to get reminded about the clause and boundaries of the authorization I have received from the design rights owner. If you don’t have both:

Root Cause:

Actions Taken:

1. We read and understood the Amazon Intellectual Property Policy. [https://sellercentral.amazon.co.uk/help/hub/reference/G201361070](https://sellercentral.amazon.co.uk/help/hub/reference/G201361070)

FAQs

1. How can sellers effectively conduct due diligence searches for design rights?

Before listing a product, sellers should check the UK Intellectual Property Office (UKIPO), the EU Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO), and the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) for registered designs. Searching involves using keywords, images, or product categories to find similar registered designs. If a design appears to be owned by someone else, sellers should seek legal guidance before listing.

2. What happens if my design is original but unregistered?

Unregistered design rights still provide legal protection, but proving ownership is harder. Sellers must demonstrate that their design was copied by providing evidence such as sketches, prototypes, or proof of first sale. If accused of infringement, presenting this evidence to Amazon and seeking legal support is recommended.

3. How can I register my design to avoid future infringement claims?

Sellers can register their designs with the UKIPO (for UK protection), EUIPO (for EU-wide protection), or other relevant national offices. The process typically involves submitting design images, a description, and an application fee. Once approved, sellers receive a design certificate granting exclusive rights.

4. How long does it take for Amazon to respond to a design rights infringement claim or appeal?

Amazon’s response time varies but typically takes 5–10 business days for an initial reply. If additional information is required, the process can extend up to 30 days or more. Monitoring the case ID and following up through Seller Central is crucial.

5. What should I do if my product is flagged for design rights infringement in one Amazon marketplace but not others?

Design rights are territorial, meaning they apply only in the regions where they are registered. If a product is flagged in Amazon UK but remains active in other marketplaces, check if the design is protected in those regions. If necessary, delist the product in the affected marketplace or obtain a licensing agreement to continue selling.

6. What can I do if I am falsely accused of design rights infringement?

If you believe the claim is false, follow these steps:

- Gather evidence proving the originality of your design, such as design sketches, invoices, or prototypes.
- Contact the complainant and request clarification or a retraction if the claim is incorrect.
- Submit a Plan of Action (POA) to Amazon, explaining why the claim is invalid and attaching supporting documents.

7. How does a design rights infringement affect my seller account health?

Infringement claims can impact Account Health metrics, potentially leading to listing removals, account warnings, or suspension if multiple violations occur. Sellers should regularly monitor their Account Health Dashboard and resolve intellectual property issues promptly to maintain good standing.

8. What are the chances of reinstating a listing removed for design rights infringement?

If a seller provides a valid design certificate or licensing agreement, the chances of reinstatement are high. However, if the seller does not have ownership or permission, reinstatement is unlikely unless they obtain authorization from the design owner.

9. How is design rights infringement different from trademark infringement?

- Design Rights: Protect the appearance of a product (e.g., shape, patterns, packaging).
- Trademark Rights: Protect brand names, logos, and symbols used to identify goods or services.
- Example: A registered design may cover the shape of a unique bottle, while a trademark would protect the logo on the bottle.
