# Catalog SOP: How to Fix Trademark (TM) Infringement Yank

## **How to Fix Trademark (TM) Infringement Yank**

**Who is this for :** This is for all individuals and sellers whose listings get yanked due to trademark (TM) infringement.

**Objective :** To outline the steps and guidelines for resolving trademark (TM) infringement issues and reinstating yanked listings.

**SUMMARY:**

Intellectual property (IP) refers to creations of the mind, such as inventions; literary and artistic works; designs; symbols, names, and images used in commerce. IP is protected in law by, for example, patents, copyright, and trademarks, which enable people to earn recognition or financial benefit from what they invent or create.[1]

A TM is a word, a group of words, signs, symbols, logos, or a combination thereof that identifies and differentiates the source of the goods or services of one entity from those of others.[2]

Amazon enforces IP policies to prevent legal involvement in IP infringements. Here are 3 main types of IP rights that Amazon observes:

- Copyrights are legal protections for original works of authorship
- Trademarks are legal protections for a word, symbol, design, or combination of the same that a company uses to identify goods and services
- Patents are legal protections for inventions

For more information, please visit this [Amazon article](https://sellercentral.amazon.com/help/hub/reference/201361070).

**Yank Reason Message:**

- We have taken this measure because we suspect your listings may infringe on the intellectual property of others. The listings are using a trademarked term on their detail pages, but their brand names do not match the rights owner of the trademarked term. This is likely to cause confusion as to the source, endorsement, or affiliation of the goods, and is a violation of the Amazon Intellectual Property Policy for Sellers, which can be reviewed at: [https://sellercentral.amazon.com/gp/help/G201361070](https://sellercentral.amazon.com/gp/help/G201361070)

NOTE: We have some cases where the sellers aren’t aware of which TMs they infringed. If this is the case for you, please jump to “How to Fix?” section IV.E.

> **Before Submitting a case, Make sure :**
>
> You have only selected the seller who should not be in the listing.
>
> You are not selecting your **OWN listing/accoun**t to be removed
>
> You have **NOT chosen** the “Remove all sellers” option.
>
> The request is not being submitted on a Friday or the day before a holiday after 12 PM EST

**HOW TO FIX IT?**

**(I.) Confirm if the Seller owns the TM.**

1. Obtain the serial number and registration number certificate (if any) of the TM from the United States Patent and Trademark Office (USPTO).
2. Go to Performance > Account Health > Received Intellectual Property Complaints > Look for the ASIN and click the Appeal button.

3. Dispute that the Seller owns the TM and attach all necessary documents as proof that they really own it.
4. If the ASIN isn’t under performance account health, then send the appeal through seller central (SC) help. Go to Help > Get Support > Selling on Amazon > Or, browse for your issue in the menu > Products, Listings, or Inventory > Inventory file upload issue. The following is an example template of the appeal:

*Greetings Amazon,*

*We’re sorry that you yanked ASIN B0XXXXXX because of TM infringement of (TM in question).*

*We want to dispute that we own the TM (name of the brand or TM ). Please see the attached USPTO certificate (or any documents that’ll prove you own the trademark).*

*Please reinstate the listing. Send this to the internal team for further review and reinstatement.*

**(II.) If Seller is Authorized by the TM Owner?**

1. Obtain a brand authorization letter, licensing agreement (LA), or letter of authorization (LOA) from the brand or TM owner. Please ask the seller for these documents.
2. Go to Performance > Account Health > Received Intellectual Property Complaints > Look for the ASIN and click the Appeal button.
3. Dispute that the Seller is authorized by the brand or TM owner and attach all necessary documents (either of the 3 mentioned documents above).
4. If the ASIN isn’t under performance account health, then send the appeal through seller central (SC) help. Go to Help > Get Support > Selling on Amazon > Or, browse for your issue in the menu > Products, Listings, or Inventory > Inventory file upload issue. The following is an example template of the appeal:

**Short description:**US - Trademark Infringement - Appeal - ASIN: B0XXXXXX

*Greetings Amazon,*

*We’re sorry that you yanked ASIN B0XXXXXX because of TM infringement of (TM in question).*

*We want to dispute that we are authorized by (who owns the TM ) to use the TM (TM in question). [Restate here what’s in the agreement or letter of authorization and reason out within the scope of the violation]. See attached (type of document).*

*Please reinstate the listing. Send this to the internal team for further review and reinstatement.*

**(III.) Seller does not own the TM or Authorized by the TM Owner**

1. If the TM is on product packaging, it is best to pull out all of the inventories that infringed TMs because it will eventually result to account deactivation if Amazon proves that the seller willfully infringed the TM.
2. TM is on product packaging continued: If the seller insists on reinstatement, you can try appealing using the manufacturer’s invoice and using the same template as shown in section II. Nevertheless, I won’t guarantee that the listing will get reinstated.

**(IV.) TM Misuse**

Sometimes, sellers identify TMs in the product descriptions and/or product images of their listings without the consent of the brand or TM owner. It is either deliberately added or added by accident. The following steps will help reinstate the listing:

**If the Seller knows the TM Infringement (Misuse)**

1. Remove all of the TM from the product description, product images, search terms, and A+ contents. Always document all of your actions and collect the batch IDs from all of the feed file updates.
2. Go to Performance > Account Health > Received Intellectual Property Complaints > Look for the ASIN and click the Appeal button.
3. Dispute that the Seller understands that he/she violated the IP policies and used the TM from his/her product descriptions but that you have removed them and that the listing is compliant with Amazon’s IP policies. Provide the batch IDs.
4. If the ASIN isn’t under performance account health, then send the appeal through seller central (SC) help. Go to Help > Get Support > Selling on Amazon > Or, browse for your issue in the menu > Products, Listings, or Inventory > Inventory file upload issue. The following is an example template of the appeal:

**Short description:** US - Trademark Infringement - Appeal - ASIN: B0xx

*Greetings Amazon,*

*We’re sorry that you yanked ASIN B0xx because of TM infringement of (TM in question).*

*We want to admit that we’re not authorized to use TM (name of brand or TM) in our product description and images.*

*To comply with Amazon’s Intellectual Property policies, I have removed [TM name] (batch ID:)*

*[Please enumerate where you have removed the trademark]*

*Please reinstate the listing. Send this to the internal team for further review and reinstatement.*

**If the Seller doesn’t Know the TM Infringement (Misuse)**

1. Go to the United States Patent and Trademark Office website and use their Trademark Electronic Search System ([TESS](https://tmsearch.uspto.gov/search/search-information)). Look for possible trademarks within the description or on the product images.
2. If you’re able to identify the TM, ask the seller if they are the authorized seller and loop back to section II. If not, loop back to section IV.B.

FAQs

1. What are Amazon’s trademark infringement policies?

Amazon prohibits sellers from using registered trademarks without permission in:

- Brand names (if the seller is not the brand owner or authorized distributor).
- Product titles, descriptions, and bullet points unless in a factual, compatibility-based statement.
- Images or packaging that falsely suggest affiliation with the trademark owner.

More details can be found in Amazon’s Intellectual Property Policy for Sellers:
[Amazon IP Policy](https://sellercentral.amazon.com/help/hub/reference/G201361070)

2. How can I determine which trademark caused my listing to be yanked?

1. Check Amazon’s Notification – Go to Performance > Account Health > Received Intellectual Property Complaints.
2. Use USPTO’s TESS Database – Search for the trademark term or brand name in the Trademark Electronic Search System (TESS).
3. Review Your Listing Content – Look for any trademarked terms in the title, bullet points, description, or images.
4. Contact Amazon Support – If Amazon’s notice is unclear, open a case asking which TM was violated.

3. How long does Amazon take to review a trademark infringement appeal?

Amazon typically reviews trademark-related reinstatement requests within 7–14 business days. If there is no response within 5 business days, sellers should follow up and escalate the case.

4. What should I do if my trademark infringement appeal is denied?

If Amazon denies reinstatement:

1. Verify that your listing no longer contains TM violations.
2. Submit a revised appeal with clearer supporting documents (proof of TM ownership or authorization).
3. Request escalation to the Amazon Legal Team via Seller Support.
4. If applicable, contact the trademark owner and request a retraction of the complaint.

5. Can I use trademarked names in my product listing?

Yes, but only in specific situations:

- Compatibility Statements – "Compatible with Apple iPhone" is allowed, but "Apple iPhone Case" is not.
- Descriptive Use – If the TM is used factually and not misleading.
- Authorization from TM Owner – A licensing agreement or brand authorization letter is required for full usage.

6. What steps should I take to prevent future trademark infringement issues?

- Check trademarks in USPTO/TESS before using brand names in listings.
- Avoid using brand names in product titles unless authorized.
- Use generic descriptions (e.g., "Wireless Earbuds" instead of "AirPods").
- Document all brand permissions and licensing agreements to defend against wrongful claims.

7. Can I sell a product if the trademark appears on the packaging?

If a trademark is on the physical product or packaging, sellers must:

- Have a reseller agreement, brand authorization, or manufacturer invoice.
- Ensure the listing does not imply brand ownership unless authorized.
- If Amazon rejects the appeal, remove infringing inventory to avoid account suspension.

8. How can I check the status of my reinstatement request?

- Go to Performance > Account Health > Received Intellectual Property Complaints.
- If your case is not listed under Account Health, check Case Log in Seller Support.
- If no update is received within 5–7 business days, follow up via chat or phone support.

9. How do I escalate a denied trademark infringement appeal?

1. Contact Account Health Support and request escalation.
2. Ask to escalate to the Amazon Legal Team.
3. If a wrongful complaint was made, request a retraction from the trademark owner.
4. If all else fails, consider modifying the listing and relisting the product.

10. What are my options if Amazon refuses to reinstate my listing?

- Modify the listing to remove all trademarked terms and reapply.
- Create a new ASIN with compliant content and relist the product.
- Sell under a different category or private-label the product.
