Understanding insurance requirements

If you are a seller based in mainland China and offering products in the US store, your commercial liability insurance policy must meet all the standard requirements listed in the Commercial Liability Insurance Requirements. This will help protect both your business and customers by addressing common coverage gaps that can lead to claim denials. Without proper insurance coverage, you may incur significant costs in connection with product liability claims.

Common reasons for claim denials include:

Product coverage gaps: When policies don't cover all products you sell
Sunset clause limitations: When policies restrict claims reported after expiration
Invalid coverage dates: When loss occurs outside policy period

Following best practices when securing your insurance policy will help you avoid these common challenges.

Deductible advancement

If your policy contains a deductible, it is a best practice if your insurer advances (pays upfront) the deductible amount when processing a claim, rather than requiring you to pay it before the claim is handled.

How it works

Insurer pays first: Your insurance provider pays all sums up to policy limits, including the deductible amount
You reimburse later: After the claim is settled, you reimburse the insurer for the deductible portion
No upfront payment barrier: This ensures claims can be processed quickly without waiting for your payment

Best practice: Deductible advancement ensures that claims can be resolved promptly without delays caused by deductible payment processing. It protects customers while giving you time to arrange reimbursement.

Example:

If you have a $1,000 deductible and a $5,000 claim:

With advancement: Insurer pays full $5,000 to settle claim; you reimburse $1,000 deductible later
Without advancement: You must pay $1,000 upfront before claim processing begins

Coverage continuity and sunset clauses

Under the Commercial Liability Insurance Requirements, you must obtain an occurrence-based policy without a sunset clause. A sunset clause (also called a reporting limitation clause) restricts your ability to report claims after your policy expires, even if the incident occurred while your policy was active. Product liability claims often arise months or years after a product is sold. If your policy contains a sunset clause, you may have no coverage when a claim is filed, even though you paid for insurance when you sold the product.

If you cannot procure an occurrence-based policy for certain product categories that are outlined in the Frequently Asked Questions, reach out to your insurer for a claims-made policy.

Example:

You sell a product in January 2025 with an active policy.
Your policy expires December 31, 2025, and contains a sunset clause.
A customer files a claim in March 2026 for the product sold in January 2025.
Result: Claim denied due to sunset clause.

Best practice: What your policy should cover

Your policy should:

Cover claims reported at any time, as long as the incident occurred during the policy period
Maintain coverage for historical sales throughout the full statute of limitations
Continue providing financial protection even after policy termination for products sold during the policy period
US claims handling capabilities

Efficient claims processing requires clear communication and timely responses. When insurance providers lack US-based claims handling capabilities, language barriers and time zone differences can delay claim resolution, potentially leading to claim denials or customer dissatisfaction.

It is best practice for your insurance provider to demonstrate:

English-language communication
Direct claim submission in English
All claim correspondence in English
Written documentation of claim decisions in English
US insurance industry standards
Response time frames consistent with US practices
Understanding of US product liability law
Familiarity with US claims processes
US-based resources
Claims processing staff in US time zones
Direct accessibility for Amazon and customers
Prompt response to verification inquiries

Best practice: When selecting an insurance provider, confirm they have:

Established US claims handling operations
English-speaking claims adjusters
Track record of processing US product liability claims

Working with insurance providers

Your insurance provider plays a critical role in ensuring you have the coverage you need when claims arise. Establishing clear communication and verification processes with your provider helps prevent coverage gaps and ensures efficient claim handling.

Best practices for working with insurance providers

When selecting and working with an insurance provider, it's beneficial to choose providers who can:

Provide required documentation
Provide certificates of insurance, the full insurance policy, or other documents Amazon may request for the coverage.
Maintain continuous coverage throughout your term as an Amazon seller without gaps.
Meet notice requirements
Ensure your insurance provider gives Amazon at least 30 days' notice of cancellation, modification, or non-renewal (as required under Section 9 of the Business Solutions Agreement, this is your obligation as the seller).
Support verification processes
Work cooperatively with Amazon's verification inquiries.
Respond promptly to coverage confirmation requests.

Best practice: Providers who work cooperatively with Amazon's verification process help streamline policy approval and reduce administrative burden for you as the seller.

Choosing the right insurance provider

Amazon Insurance Accelerator

The Amazon Insurance Accelerator offers pre-vetted insurance providers who meet all requirements and offer competitive rates. Benefits include:

Automatic verification (no manual submission needed)
Broader coverage through excluded products model
Standardized claim handling
Regular policy status updates

Non-panel providers

If you choose a provider outside the Insurance Accelerator, ensure they provide a policy that complies with the Commercial Liability Insurance Requirements. Additionally, as a best practice, the insurance provider should:

Have established US claims handling capabilities
Be able to provide required documentation and verification
Understand Amazon's insurance requirements
