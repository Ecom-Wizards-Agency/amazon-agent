Search optimization

Customers must find your products to be able to buy them. This guide explains how to accurately classify and list your products to optimize them for search.

Customers who shop on Amazon use keywords or browse refinements to find what they're looking for. By providing relevant and complete information for your product, you may increase your product's discoverability, which could lead to increased sales, so it’s important that your products are classified accurately in Browse. Product information like title, brand, product description, and other attributes must all be accurate and compliant with detail page rules. Any products that don’t comply with our guidelines may be suppressed from search until the quality issues are resolved.

Note: We reserve the right to not use all supplied content from the product description, including bullet points, for retrieving products in customers' shopping search results. Reasons for not using all keywords may include, but are not limited to, search computational efficiency, potential manipulation of shopping results, irrelevant search terms, and offensive or illegal terms. However, it's recommended to keep these details updated as per the style guidelines.

Search and browse

Customers can discover products by browsing or searching. Most often customers use a combination of browse and search.

Browse

To help customers find products easily, Amazon developed a detailed product hierarchy or browse-tree structure. Customers refine by category and subcategory links until they reach the most specific product type. Amazon uses Item Type Keywords to classify products under the correct browse nodes.

Accurate product classification optimizes discoverability, making it easier for Amazon to help customers find what they're looking for through browsing and searching. Classifying products is like placing your products in the correct aisle and on the correct shelf in the store. When customers search in All Departments, Amazon may limit the shopping results to products that match specific category classifications or product descriptors.

Selecting and applying browse keywords

Start with the item to classify and refer to Classify your products using Browse Tree Guides - BTG to learn about Browse Tree Guides. Browse Tree Guides are category-specific documents that provide specific keywords for your products.

Note: The item type keyword must have the same spelling and formatting as listed in the Browse Tree Guides. Make sure to select keywords at the most specific subcategory available.

Example: If you're selling women's running shoes:

Too broad: "Shoes" or "Athletic shoes"
Correct: "Women's road running shoes"

Search and generic keywords

For customers to find your products on Amazon, it's important to provide search terms that they might use when searching for what they want to buy. Search terms are keywords that are used by the search engine for lexical matching between an ASIN and a query. For example, if you’re listing a stainless steel refrigerator, you could include terms like fridge, freezer, icebox, cooler, energy-efficient, frost-free, adjustable shelves, temperature control, stainless steel, kitchen, household, French door, and ice maker.

It's important that you provide honest details about your product, including compatibility for electronics or functionality for appliances.

You can add search terms for your products. These search terms should only include generic words that enhance the discoverability of your product. For example, if you're selling headphones, your search terms can contain synonyms such as "earphones" and "earbuds." These are not visible on product detail pages, and can be updated in the Generic Keyword field of your listing.

For more information on Search, visit Use search terms effectively.

Note: We limit the length of the Generic Keyword attribute to less than 250 bytes. Search terms can be eliminated if prohibited search terms such as brand names, ASINs, or offensive language are supplied as generic keywords.

Search terms best practices

Below are some best practices to optimize your listings for search:

Maintain the text length limit of search terms
Make use of synonyms and avoid repeating keywords
Use spelling variations, and avoid common misspellings
Include abbreviations
Use all lowercase letters
Separate words with spaces, and avoid using punctuation marks such as semicolons, colons, and dashes
Use either singular or plural words, don’t use both singular and plural forms of the same keyword
Don’t use brand names, ASINs, profanity, or offensive terms
Avoid using articles, prepositions, and other short words like “a”, “and,” “or,” “the,” or “with”
Don’t use temporary statements like “new” or “on sale now,” or subjective claims like “best” or “cheapest”
Note: Violating these rules may result in your ASIN being suppressed and may put the health of your selling account at risk.

Understanding search behavior

Search query automatic correction

An ASIN may sometimes be unsearchable due to an automatic correction of the search query. When a search query gets corrected, the customer will see a Showing Results message with the corrected query at the top of the results. There will also be a Search instead for [original query] link for cases where the spell-corrected query isn't helpful for the customer and they want to view the results for their original query.

ASIN position fluctuation

We don’t guarantee a specific placement for an ASIN within search results. ASIN positions can fluctuate as we continuously strive to present the most relevant results to customers.

If the position of an ASIN is changing, it may be due to the location of their search, the type of account they are logged in to, or whether they’re on desktop or mobile. You may not see the ASIN in the same position as reported by the Search Troubleshooter tool. This is expected behavior.

Search result limitations

Amazon limits the number of ASINs shown on the search results pages and does not show results after a certain number of pages, depending on the type of search and number of results per page.

If the ASIN doesn’t appear within the set limit of results, it won’t be possible to navigate to it via the search query using the page numbers or next page links at the bottom of the search result page. Customers can use a more specific query or use filters to refine the results to discover the ASIN in the search.

Search Troubleshooting tool

The goal of search troubleshooting is to help you understand your product discoverability issues. If manual search troubleshooting tips haven't helped diagnose the search issues, you can use a tool called Determine why an ASIN is not searchable.

Note: Access this tool by searching for ‘ASIN is unsearchable' within Seller Central help. Input the Search URL, ASIN (child/non-parent ASIN), and the tool will diagnose why the ASIN is not searchable.
