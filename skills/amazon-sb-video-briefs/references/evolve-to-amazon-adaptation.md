# Evolve to Amazon: the SB video adaptation

Source: the Evolve playbook (Meta direct-response creative system). This file is the translation layer. It keeps what survives the move from Meta to Amazon and replaces what does not. Read this before writing any Sponsored Brands video script or editor brief.

## 1. The core reframe: push vs pull

Meta is push. The viewer did not ask for the ad. The ad interrupts, earns attention, and walks the viewer down awareness stages inside one 45-second video.

Amazon is pull. The shopper typed a query. They are mid-decision, on a results page full of near-identical competitors. The video does not create demand. It wins a comparison that is already happening.

Three consequences:

1. There is no unaware or problem-aware traffic on Amazon search. The lowest awareness you will ever meet is solution-aware. Every Evolve technique for unaware hooks (stories, secrets, infotainment, hidden fear) is off the table. Do not use them.
2. The scroll-stop is replaced by the SERP-win. The video autoplays muted, small, in a row of static tiles. Its job in the first 2 seconds: prove relevance to the exact query and give one reason to look at us instead of the tile above and below.
3. Congruence beats curiosity. On Meta an open loop holds attention. On Amazon an open loop reads as irrelevance and the eye moves on. Front-load everything. The message must land inside the first 5 seconds even if nothing else is watched.

## 2. What transfers from Evolve unchanged

- The Rule of One. One avatar, one desire, one message. Amazon version: one query intent, one buying criterion, one video. Never make "the brand video" that answers every query.
- The execution problem. Right angle plus bad words or bad visuals still equals a dead ad. If a video fails, ask execution-vs-strategy before killing the angle: test the same angle in a second execution before moving on.
- Show, don't tell. Stronger on Amazon than on Meta because the sound is off. Drop the phone case, wipe the pan, pour the shake. People cannot disagree with what they see, and demos need no audio.
- Market sophistication. Amazon niches are usually stage 3 to 5: every competitor already makes the same claim. The counter is the same as in Evolve: a new mechanism, an enlarged mechanism, or identification. Check what the top 10 tiles for the query already say before choosing the claim.
- Concept, angle, variation, format vocabulary. Keep it. A brief without an angle underneath the concept gets rejected, same rule as Evolve.
- Marksman then Sniper. Test 3 hook variants on one shared hold to find direction, then iterate deeply on the winner.
- Feedback loops. Complete learnings on every video before writing the next brief. The learnings questions are adapted in section 9.
- The customer's own words. Mine reviews and the query corpus itself. The query is literally the customer typing their desire. Use its exact wording in the on-screen text.

## 3. What gets replaced

| Evolve (Meta) | Amazon SB replacement |
|---|---|
| Avatar research from Reddit, TikTok, ad comments | Query-cluster research from POE, DataDive, SQP, plus review mining for language |
| 5 awareness levels, walk them all in one ad | Query type sets the entry stage; only stages 3 to 5 exist |
| Hook fights for attention against entertainment | Hook fights for relevance against 5 competing tiles |
| 43 to 45 seconds, VO-led, sound on | 15 to 30 seconds, text-led, designed for mute; audio is a bonus layer |
| Hook, Bridge, Hold, CTA | Frame-1 proof, Differentiator, Proof block, Brand lockup (section 6) |
| CTA drives to a landing page you control | CTA is soft; the click lands on the listing, so ad-to-listing congruence replaces ad-to-lander congruence |
| Meta benchmarks: hook rate 25%, CTR 1% | SB video benchmarks: judge CTR against the account's SB baseline and CVR against the ASIN's Business Report CVR (section 9) |
| Compliance: watch "root cause" claims | Compliance is a hard layer: Amazon creative policy plus health-claims regimes (section 8) |

## 4. Query-to-awareness map

The query replaces the avatar as the unit of targeting. Classify every target cluster before scripting:

| Query type | Example | Stage | Video job |
|---|---|---|---|
| Generic category | "kollagen pulver" | Solution-aware | Differentiate on mechanism or form. Show the product doing the thing. One claim, proven visually. |
| Attribute long-tail | "collagen powder no additives" | Solution-aware with a stated criterion | Mirror the criterion in frame 1 text, verbatim. Then prove it. |
| Problem long-tail | "supplement for joint pain"* | Problem-to-solution boundary | Lead with the outcome for that problem, within claims policy. |
| Competitor brand | "vital proteins collagen" | Product-aware, conquesting | Never name the competitor. Position against the category default: "typical collagen does X, this does Y." |
| Own brand | "sheko kollagen" | Most-aware | SKIP. Operator rule (2026-07-23): no videos for branded keywords. Branded clusters never enter the shortlist. |

*Problem queries in health niches are where claims risk concentrates. Flag them in the brief.

One video per query cluster. A cluster is a set of queries sharing one intent and one buying criterion, not one root. "collagen powder" and "collagen supplement" can share a video. "collagen for skin" and "collagen for joints" cannot.

## 5. Choosing which videos to make: the data layer

This is the part the playbook cannot give you. Build the shortlist from data, not taste.

Inputs, in priority order:

1. POE (niche level): top search terms, search volume, click share and conversion share of the top ASINs. Find queries with high volume where the top 3 do not dominate click share: those are winnable shelves.
2. DataDive (niche keyword grid): relevancy, our rank per query, root aggregation. Use it to draw cluster boundaries and to check whether we have organic support (a video that clicks to a listing that does not mention the criterion will not convert).
3. SQP (per-ASIN): our real impression, click, and purchase share per query. The video shortlist lives where impression share is meaningful but click share underperforms: we are seen and not chosen. That is a creative problem, which a video can fix.
4. AdLabs or bulk ads data: where we already spend on the cluster and what SB or SP CTR and CVR look like today. An existing high-spend, low-CTR SB target is the single best video candidate in the account.

Scorecard per candidate cluster (gate, then rank):

- Gates: search volume above the niche's long-tail floor; offer actually fits the query intent; listing supports the claim; no unresolvable compliance block; never a branded (own-brand) cluster: no videos for branded keywords (operator rule).
- Rank by: spend already flowing to the cluster, volume, gap between impression share and click share, margin headroom vs break-even ACOS.

Output: 3 to 5 clusters max per batch. One excellent brief a day beats five rushed ones, same rule as Evolve.

## 6. The script skeleton

Target 15 to 30 seconds. Amazon allows up to 45; almost nothing on a SERP earns 45. The message must be complete at 5 seconds, satisfying at 20.

| Block | Time | Job | Rules |
|---|---|---|---|
| Frame-1 proof | 0 to 2s | Prove relevance to the query, muted, at thumbnail size | Product visible and in action in frame 1. On-screen text mirrors the query language. No logo intro, no fade-in, no empty pack shot, no title card. Start mid-action. |
| Differentiator | 2 to 10s | The one reason to pick us for this query | One claim only, the cluster's buying criterion. New mechanism or enlarged mechanism per sophistication check. Show it, then caption it. |
| Proof block | 10 to 20s | Make the claim safe to believe | Compressed OCPB: claim already made, now proof (demo, texture, numbers with substantiation, unique numerals) and benefit shown in life. Secondary benefit at most. |
| Brand lockup | 20 to 30s | Memory and soft CTA | Pack shot, brand mark, variant range if cross-sell matters. The video loops: end on a frame that reads as a clean restart, or cut so the loop is seamless. |

Hook variants: write 3 Frame-1-plus-Differentiator openings (A, B, C) over one shared Proof block and lockup. That is the Marksman batch. Variants must differ in angle, not wording: for example A mirrors the attribute, B shows the outcome, C attacks the category default.

Writing rules carried over from Evolve, tightened for Amazon:

- Grade-6 language or below, in the customer's words from reviews and queries.
- Unique numerals beat round numbers, but any number on screen needs substantiation that survives Amazon moderation.
- No bossy claims, no empty superlatives. "The best" is both weak copy and a policy violation.
- Visual language: every scripted line must name what is on screen. A line the editor cannot film is not a line.

## 7. Sound-off design rules

The video autoplays muted and small. Editors who come from Meta will get this wrong unless the brief says it:

- Every information beat exists as picture plus on-screen text. Voiceover and music are enhancement only.
- Text: max ~7 words per card, one card at a time, high contrast, inside safe margins, legible at roughly 300px wide. If it fails the thumbnail squint test, it fails.
- Reading time: a card stays on screen roughly 1 second per 12 characters plus a beat for the visual, minimum 2.5 seconds. Time every card against this before fixing slot lengths; when a card runs over its slot, extend the slot, do not shrink the text (operator preference 2026-07-23). Longer translations extend the card too.
- Visual metaphors must be literally true: no fake unbranded-competitor shelf scenes or staged comparison rows (operator cut one 2026-07-23: it "doesn't make sense" when the shopper knows no real shelf looks like that). Show the product's own proof instead.
- 16:9, 1920x1080, MP4 or MOV, under 500MB, under 45s. No letterboxing, no pillarboxing. An audio track is still required by spec (min 96 kbps), even though default playback is muted.
- No end-card URLs, no social handles, no "click the link": off-platform directives are a policy rejection.
- Language of the marketplace: DE videos carry German on-screen text, not English.

## 8. Compliance: the hard layer Meta does not have

Amazon moderates ad creative before it serves, and health claims carry regulatory risk beyond moderation (HCVO in the EU, FTC and FDA framing in the US).

Policy floor for every video (rejection triggers):

- No cure, treat, prevent, or disease claims for supplements and cosmetics.
- No "#1", "best", "leading" without on-screen third-party substantiation and date.
- No price, discount, or urgency claims inside the video file.
- No competitor names or marks. No review quotes unless verbatim, attributed, and unedited.
- No before-and-after imagery for weight loss or disease-adjacent outcomes.

Health-claims gate (advisory mode): run the /health-claims-check flow on the full script text plus every on-screen text card before the brief ships. The brief carries a per-line risk table (LOW, MEDIUM, HIGH) with a suggested compliant rewrite next to every MEDIUM and HIGH line. The operator decides per line. The editor never resolves claims wording alone.

Claims survive the move down, not up: a line that is compliant as visible listing copy is usually safe in a video. A line that was backend-only in the keyword workbook must never appear on screen.

## 9. The testing loop

Launch:

- 1 SB video campaign per cluster, exact and phrase on the cluster's queries, nothing else in the campaign. The campaign is the test cell.
- Run the A, B, C hook variants as separate ads in the campaign where the account supports multiple video ads; otherwise rotate sequentially with equal flighting.
- Set bids to actually win the video slot: an untested video at a starvation bid produces no learnings.

Read (after meaningful spend, not before roughly 2 weeks or a click volume worth reading):

| Metric | Compare against | What it tells you |
|---|---|---|
| CTR | The account's SB baseline and the cluster's SP CTR | Frame-1 and hook quality: are we chosen on the shelf |
| VTR (5s views / impressions) | Own account history | Hook hold, the Amazon cousin of Evolve's hook rate |
| CVR | The ASIN's Business Report CVR for the same period | Ad-to-listing congruence: did the video promise what the listing sells |
| ACOS vs break-even | Client's real margin | Whether the cluster scales |
| SQP click share, before vs after | Same query, prior period | The shelf-level win, the metric the whole system exists to move |

Verdict rules, Evolve-adapted:

- High CTR, low CVR: the video wins the click but breaks congruence. Fix the listing or the promise, not the hook.
- Low CTR, decent CVR on the clicks that come: frame-1 problem. Sniper-iterate the opening only, keep the hold.
- All variants flat after honest spend: angle problem, not execution. Re-run cluster selection; the criterion you led with is not the buying criterion.
- A winner: scale bids and coverage, then Marksman a new angle batch on the next cluster. Do not iterate the winner into overload.

Learnings questions per video (the adapted checklist):

1. Does frame 1 show the product doing the thing, muted, at thumbnail size?
2. Does the first text card mirror the query language of the cluster?
3. Could a shopper get the full message at 5 seconds?
4. Is there exactly one claim, and is it different from the top 10 tiles?
5. Would every scripted line survive Amazon moderation and the claims table?
6. Does the listing deliver what the video promises?
7. What did the previous winner teach, and where is it applied here?
